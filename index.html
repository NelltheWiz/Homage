Dr. Valerie L. Thomas

An influential African American scientist and inventor whose work at NASA and her invention of the illusion transmitter have left a lasting impact on technology and science.

The Illusion Transmitter was a precursor to modern 3D movie technology, demonstrating the possibility of creating and transmitting 3D images.

Here's the timeline of Dr. Valerie Thomas life.

  • 1943 - Valerie Laverne Thomas was born February 1st in Baltimore Maryland and raised in the prominently black Cherry Hill community
  • 1949 - Valerie's interest in science began when she'd watch her father tinkering with the television and seeing all the mechanical parts inside the TV.
  • 1951 - At the tender age of eight, Valerie read "The Boys' First Book on Electronics" which sparked her interest in a career in science.
  • 1964 - Graduated from Morgan State University, majored in Physics(one of only two women in the program)
  • 1964 - Began working at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center as a data analyst.
  • 1970 - Developed real-time computer data systems to support satellite operations control centers
  • 1974 - Led a team of approximately 50 people for the Large Area Crop Inventory Experiment (LACIE), a joint effort with NASA's Johnson Space Center, NOAA, and the U.S. Department of Agriculture.
  • 1976 - Attended a science exhibition that included an illusion of a light bulb that appeared to be lit, even though it had been removed from its socket. This inspired her to begin researching optical illusions.
  • 1977 - Began experimenting with concave mirrors to create optical illusions, leading to her invention of the illusion transmitter.
  • 1980 - On October 21st, Valerie received a patent for the illusion transmitter, a device that creates 3D illusions using concave mirrors.
  • 1986 - Until 1990 Valerie served as the Space Physics Analysis Network (SPAN) project manager, during which SPAN underwent a major reconfiguration and expanded from approximately 100 computer nodes to about 2,700 worldwide.
  • 1990 - SPAN became a major part of NASA's science networking and today's Internet.
  • 1995 - Retired from NASA at the end of August, concluding her service as associate chief of the Space Science Data Operations Office, manager of the NASA Automated Systems Incident Response Capability, and chair of the Space Science Data Operations Office Education Committee.
